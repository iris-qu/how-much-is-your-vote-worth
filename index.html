To maximize your political influence in the United States,
we suggest you to:

  • Vote in the general election

    If this is your top result, it means that you live in either a swing state (Ohio, Colorado, Iowa, Nevada, New Hampshire, Virginia, and Florida) or you live in Maine or Nebraska, whose electoral college does not operate on a winner-take-all system. Basically, compared to people in the other states, your vote actually can make a big difference. It’s not only your right to vote, but, because of your special power here, it’s actually an honor. So, for the sake of all your homies in other states whose votes really don’t make much of a difference, make an informed decision! We need you!

  • Vote in primary/caucus

    ________

    Okay, so if this is your top result, you’re either in a swing state, OR you live in a state with open primaries. Open primary voting allows citizens who are registered to vote but did not register with either of the major political parties, so you could be a devoted independent, or getting scrappy on the ground with the green party; no matter how you’ve registered, you get to have a say in which candidates get to the final round of the presidential race, which is pretty damn important. So, just as if this were the final, general election, get informed! You are in a unique position here where you can advance the best candidate that you think is out there. Check the primary schedule and figure out when you’re going to get your voice heard:

  • Vote in senate and house election

    ________

    As you know by now, most states are not swing states, so you are part of the majority if you get this answer as your top result. Most of us are somewhat blind to our local elections, because the media covers the presidential election so much more heavily than others. But, as we’ve seen in the past few years especially, our congresspeople are actually very directly involved in legislature that affects our day-to-day lives, and we should be putting them under the same microscope as our presidential candidates, which so few Americans actually do. In 2014, only 36.4% of registered voters turned out for midterm elections, a record low for the last 70 years. If your state made the list, vote in house election this year!
